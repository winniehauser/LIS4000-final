Children's Librarianship

Diversity in Children's Libraries

Diversity is hugely important in the children's library space. According to research done by the ALSC, "it is essential that children learn to understand the important role of their culture and the cultures of other people in creating an overall global culture respectful of differences." The library is a great way for children to gain an awareness of other cultures as well as their own. Additionally, "culturally authentic materials in library collections allow all children to meet people like themselves and develop an appreciation for the beauty of their culture and the cultures of others," demonstrating why representation in library materials is so important (Naidoo, 2014).

Children want to see themselves represented in the library's materials, and "when children never see their culture represented in a library storytime or in materials on the library shelves, they received a resounding message that the librarian does not think their culture is important enough to feature in the library" (Naidoo, 2014).

According to the Cooperative Children's Book Center at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, only about 8% of children's books that are published feature non-white cultures (American Indians, Asian Americans, Latinos, and African Americans). As children's librarians, it is up to us to include and showcase books published by diverse authors and about diverse cultures in order to allow more children to feel and be represented in library materials, thus contributing to a more equitable world.

"By including diversity in its programs and collections, the library has the potential for helping children make cross-cultural connections and develop the skills necessary to function in a culturally pluralistic society." (Naidoo, 2014)
