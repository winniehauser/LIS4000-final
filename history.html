Children's Librarianship

History of the Field

Children's Librarianship originated in the 1940s and is accredited to the work of Anne Carroll Moore with the New York Public Library. She was a pioneer of children's libraries and was present when the first child received a library card. Previously, there had simply not been a space for children in the library, as it was geared toward students and adults, and remained what is stereotypically thought of as a silent place for study and reading (Lareau, 2021). Children's libraries, at the start, emphasized a love for reading and wanted to encourage young readers in the community. These libraries in America inspired libraries in other countries; wealthy Americans helped to build libraries in Europe in the early 1920s as well.

The emphasis in children's libraries is often on “a belief in the uniqueness of each child; a belief in the crucial importance of each child's personal selection of reading materials; a belief in the children's room as "an egalitarian republic of readers;" and a belief in literature as a positive force for understanding, not only between individuals, but also between groups and nations” (Children's Libraries | Encyclopedia.com, n.d.). In today's society, there are many libraries worldwide that are solely for children, rather than a room or area inside of a traditional library. The importance of children's libraries continues to grow and be studied.


Some Children's Librarian Statistics (ALSC Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion Task Force, 2016)

93% of children's librarians are women, and 85% are white.

69% of children's librarians have an ALA-accredited Master's Degree.

79% of children's librarians are straight or heterosexual.

As children's librarians, it is up to us to make the library an inclusive space. We could definitely use some diversity amongst the librarians, as well.
